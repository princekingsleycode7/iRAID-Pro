The Challenge

Life Before Clean Water

For years, the people of Umualanchara Amizi faced daily challenges with access to clean, safe water — a basic necessity essential to healthy living and community development.

Community members depended on rainwater and a distant seasonal stream that dried up during the dry season. For many families, obtaining water meant travelling long distances before dawn, spending hours fetching from open, contaminated sources.

The stream fetching method resulted in widespread waterborne diseases including diarrhoea, typhoid, and skin infections — particularly affecting women and children who bore the burden of collection.

Construction

How It Was Built

From site mapping to pump testing — a rigorous, community-involved construction process.

01

Mapping & Preparation

The perimeter fencing area was professionally mapped out and the site was fully prepared before drilling commenced. Community members were briefed and engaged from the outset.

02

Drilling

Mechanised rotary drilling was carried out to a depth of approximately 137 metres. Soil samples were collected at regular intervals to assess geology and water quality.

03

Casing & Compressing

The borehole was professionally cased and compressed to secure the well structure and prevent surface contamination from entering the water column.

04

Pump Testing & Commissioning

Riser pipes were installed and pump testing conducted to confirm water yield and quality. Community members gathered to witness the first flow — an emotional milestone for Umualanchara Amizi.

People-Centred

Community Involvement

From construction to ownership — the community of Umualanchara Amizi is at the heart of every step.

🤝

Local Participation

Community members actively participated during construction, supporting site work and local logistics throughout the project timeline.

🎓

Training Provided

Women were trained on hygiene and basic maintenance of the solar water scheme (April 15–17, 2026, at Umuifeze Civic Hall). Additional virtual training by the Grundfos Technical Team is planned for the Project Management Committee.

👨‍👩‍👧

Community Ownership

A local Water Management Committee has been established and will be trained by the Grundfos Technical Team to oversee post-commission daily operation, ensuring long-term sustainability and accountability.

Positive Impact

Measured Outcomes

  • Waterborne diseases drastically reduced in the community
  • Women and children spend significantly less time fetching water
  • Dry-season farming and small businesses are now possible
  • Schools and health facilities have reliable water access
  • Girls and women no longer travel risky paths to distant streams at night
  • Community members expressed deep joy at the first flow during pump testing

Sustainability

The Road Ahead

🔑 Local Management Committee

The Community Management Committee will be trained by the Grundfos Technical Team to oversee post-commission daily operations, ensuring the facility remains functional for generations.

🔧 Structured Maintenance Plan

A structured maintenance plan is in place, with trained community members responsible for routine upkeep — reducing dependency on external technical support.

📷 CCTV Monitoring

The installed CCTV system will enable remote monitoring and long-term accountability of the solar water infrastructure, protecting the community's investment.

Next Steps

  1. Monitor system performance for the first 12 months post-completion
  2. Support the Community Management Committee with capacity building, especially for women
  3. Explore expansion of additional boreholes in Abia State
  4. Document and share lessons learned with partner organisations
  5. Approach government for inclusion and participation in rural water policy frameworks
